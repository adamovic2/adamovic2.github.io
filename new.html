The research project
I want to look at the mechanisms of inclusion and exclusion of lesbian gay, bisexual, transsexual, intersexual and queer (LGBTIQ) migrants in Austria, who fled/migrated from Serbia or Bosnia. I am curious about the way people experience exclusion from and inclusion in communities based on sexual orientation and/or migration. 
 
Considering the influences of the EU accession of Serbia has on the discourses and policies on LGBTIQ people from Serbia, I want to look closer at the discourse of LGBT activism in Serbia and Austria. Especially the Belgrade Pride Parade is shaping the national and (concerning the EU) supranational discourse of tolerance of LGBTIQ people in Serbia as well as (but differently) in the diaspora. 
 
I want to connect these discourses to specific stereotypes and misconceptions which are still present in Austria about the “Balkan”. Looking into the queer theory, it is significant to emphasize how specific “western” discourses on the topic of sexual orientation are shaping the view, that the “backwarded” “global South” is prohibiting these liberal thoughts.
Slađana Adamović

My name is Slađana Adamović. I was born in Carinthia (Austria) and I am currently 24 years old. My parents moved seperately to Austria and met each other in Carinthia. 
 
Together we often visited my grandparents in Serbia, because after the Yugoslav war they moved from Kosovo and Bosnia to different parts in Serbia. 
 
I moved to Vienna 2014 to study cultural and social anthropology at the university of Vienna and I fell in love with the city. Here I was able to join LGBTIQ events and visited LGBTIQ organization and connected with some there too. 
 
Because of my friends and my diaspora life, I wanted to look deeper onto the experiences and stories of LGBTIQ migrants from Serbia and Bosnia in Austria, because it seemed that they always fell out of simple categories.


Literature and further information
Imagining the Balkans
Detailed examination of stereotypes and misconceptions about the regional context of the “Balkan” by Todorova shows that the representation of the “Balkan” has a history and does not evolve and exist in a vacuum. For this reason, I would like to build up on her work to analyse the discourses from a particular “Western” perspective – the Austrian one – and look how the stereotypes affect the collaborations between the LGBTIQ activism of the countries as well as how Serbia as a nation is perceived in a specific way because of its geopolitical situation. Based on the work of Said, Todorova is showing how the stereotypes of the “Balkan” developed and transformed through time and space.

Terrorist Assamblages
Not only the people but also the theory on LGBTIQ rights (queer theory) itself are affected by these images. As Kulpa and Mizielinska (2011) are pointing out, there exists a specific “Western” discourse on the topic of sexual orientation and an image, that the “backward” “Global South” is prohibiting these liberal thoughts. This is exactly where my work should attach the debate. Puar (2007) is dealing with the relationship of queerness and war considering the discursive and material strategies queer Muslims and queer Arabs in the U.S. are using to resist state and societal violence. For this manner, she is referring to Delleuze, Mbembe and Sacks to include the temporality aspect in the demarcation processes of queer migrants. The BPP in the year 2001 as a turning point of radical change in the discourse will be highlighted in the same manner as Puar involved the metaphor of 9/11, which reflects some specific spatial and temporal narratives and produces spatial and temporal discourses (Puar 2007: xviii).
